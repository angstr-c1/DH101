Week 7 – Mapping AI Worlds

The Artifact

Reflection

Research Documentation

For my research of these locations, I primarily used data that the companies themselves provided. However, most of these maps, like the one available on datacenters.google, don’t provide exact locations or addresses of these data centers. Because of this, I used multiple strategies. Because a lot of these data centers are in rural areas, they are easy to spot through a satellite image because they are often the only large building in the areas. For some of the other google data centers in the United States, I also would use images of the front of the buildings to see the address when it wasn’t available through a web search. OpenAI, on the other hand, was much more difficult to find because of its newer and more understated presence. For this, I used the website Baxtel to find the locations of data centers. However, it wasn’t the most accurate, so I cross listed it by looking at satellite and street view data.

Another problem with looking at OpenAI’s infrastructure was that, due to it being a relatively new company, they haven’t had as enormous of an expansion as Google. Because of this, they not only had less data centers under their name, but they also had much less documentation on their entire infrastructure. Another thing that was very difficult to find for both companies was the centers in more rural areas. My interpretation of this is that they are trying to hide as many data centers as possible from the public in order to not face backlash on their consumption of the natural resources of each area. This would also reveal that these companies seek out areas where the land price is very inexpensive and have large quantities of natural resources (like rivers to cool the servers). However, because of this, they also end up raising the cost of living and damaging the natural resources for the people in the area.

Artist Statement

My map is essentially showing the disconnect between AI data centers and the headquarters of the companies that own and operate them. Nearly every headquarter or corporate office for these companies are located in a major city, typically in a wealthy district. On the other hand, data centers are almost entirely in rural or cheaper industrial areas where the companies can exploit both the land and its local people in order to create a larger profit margin. This directly ties into the idea of Data Feminism because it gives a representation to underrepresented areas of the country (e.g. Nebraska). On top of this, it shows how Silicon Valley technology companies can have a large effect on the rest of the country, and by extent, the rest of the world.

Another important detail to mention is that these companies don’t have a lot of infrastructure in Africa or the Middle-East, creating a greater disparity between the “western world” and these areas. On top of this, the only infrastructure for data centers and corporate offices in Asia is in the more “westernized” or “western ally” countries like Japan or Singapore. By mapping something else, I would have focused on the mining and cheap factory labor in these regions to further show the disparity. However, I feel as though it would have taken away from the idea of showing what it has done domestically in less-represented American areas (i.e. taking away resources from rural areas and making it harder for working-class Americans to live).
